Sir Chandrasekhara Venkata Raman

C. V. Raman, full name Sir Chandrasekhara Venkata Raman

C. V. Raman, was a renowned Indian physicist who made significant contributions to the field of science and was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1930. Born on November 7, 1888, in the city of Tiruchirapalli (now known as Trichy) in the state of Tamil Nadu, India, Raman's early life was marked by a strong interest in the natural world and a fascination with science.

His exceptional work in the scientific community earned him numerous accolades, including the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1930, making him the first Asian and first Indian to receive such an honor. C. V. Raman's contributions to science not only brought international recognition to India but also inspired a new generation of scientists to pursue research and innovation in the country.

Notable Works

C. V. Raman made numerous notable contributions to the field of physics and scientific research throughout his career. Some of his most significant works include:

Raman Effect (1928):

Raman's most famous and groundbreaking work was the discovery of the "Raman Effect".

Studies in Acoustics:

C. V. Raman conducted significant research in acoustics, particularly the physics of stringed musical instruments.

Other Works

Apart from his most notable works, C. V. Raman made several other significant contributions to the field of science. Some of his other notable works include:

Molecular Diffraction of Light

Before his discovery of the Raman Effect, Raman conducted research on the molecular diffraction of light. He studied the phenomenon of light scattering and diffraction by molecules, providing valuable insights into the interaction of light with matter at the molecular level.

Optics of Colloids" (1922)

In collaboration with his student Nagendra Nath, Raman conducted research on the optics of colloids, which are tiny particles suspended in a medium. They studied the behavior of light when passing through colloidal suspensions, leading to important findings related to light scattering and its interaction with small particles.

Acoustics and the Physiology of Hearing" (1934)

Raman delved into the field of acoustics and human hearing physiology. He investigated how the human ear perceives sound and made contributions to the understanding of auditory sensations. His research provided insights into the mechanisms behind hearing and sound perception.

Early life and education

Early life

Chandrasekhara Venkata Raman, known as C. V. Raman, was born on November 7, 1888, in the city of Tiruchirapalli (also known as Trichy) in the Madras Presidency of British India (now Tamil Nadu, India). He was the second of eight children in his family. Raman's father, Chandrasekhara Iyer, was a lecturer in mathematics and physics, which had a significant influence on young Raman's interest in science.

Early Education:

Raman received his early education in Thiruvanaikaval, near Trichy. He showed exceptional academic abilities from a young age and had a deep curiosity about the natural world. His father played a crucial role in nurturing his scientific interests.

Higher Education

In 1902, at the age of 13, Raman entered the Presidency College in Madras (now Chennai) for his higher education. He excelled in his studies and particularly stood out in physics and mathematics. At the Presidency College, Raman's passion for science grew, and he began conducting small scientific experiments even as an undergraduate student.

Further Studies in London

Raman was awarded a scholarship in 1907, which allowed him to travel to England for further studies. In 1907, he joined the University of Cambridge to pursue a degree in physics at the Cavendish Laboratory, where he studied under the renowned scientists J.J. Thomson and Lord Rayleigh. In 1911, Raman received his Ph.D. degree from the University of London for his research on "Diffraction of Light" and was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society (FRS) in the same year.
